# Spike: Lurie enforcement shift — what the data shows

**Status:** Partially confirmed. SOMA, Mission, and Tenderloin deltas validated against compare-mode output. Year-over-year baseline comparison pending. World Cup 2026 chapter pending (data not yet complete as of July 2026).

**We learned that after Mayor Lurie took office, police-initiated enforcement spiked sharply in SOMA (+79.0) and Mission (+43.0) while Tenderloin — historically the most heavily drug-enforced neighborhood in SF — came in at only +29.4, suggesting a geographic reallocation of enforcement rather than a citywide increase. Therefore LENS's most compelling demo is not "crime went up" but "enforcement moved," and the World Cup window is the natural second chapter of the same story.**

---

## What Lens 2 measures (plain language)

Lens 2 is the ratio of **police-initiated stops** (drug stops, warrants, prostitution) to **serious crimes reported by residents** (burglary, assault, robbery, vehicle theft).

A rising ratio means proactive enforcement is growing faster than — or independent of — what residents are actually reporting as crimes. It is a direct read of enforcement behavior, not underlying crime.

---

## The Lurie finding

**Comparison window:** Before = Apr 2024–Dec 2024 / After = Jan 2025–Sep 2025

| Neighborhood | Delta | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| South of Market | +79.0 | ~4–6× historical year-over-year baseline |
| Mission | +43.0 | ~5× historical baseline |
| Tenderloin | +29.4 | Positive — but historically the most-enforced neighborhood in SF |

**The Tenderloin number is the key.** At +29.4 it is a real increase, but Tenderloin's own historical baseline is much higher than other neighborhoods because it has been the center of SF drug enforcement for decades. So +29.4 for Tenderloin is comparatively modest while +79 for SOMA is extraordinary — that neighborhood's baseline is much lower.

If this were a citywide crackdown, Tenderloin would have led. Instead enforcement spiked hardest in SOMA and Mission — exactly where Lurie's encampment clearing operations were concentrated. Officers appear to have moved with the encampments.

---

## What this connects to (for the demo)

Mayor Lurie took office January 2025 and immediately declared a shelter emergency, pushing aggressive encampment clearings in SOMA and Mission where the largest tent cities were concentrated. The enforcement ratio data follows that geography precisely.

**What LENS shows:** the pattern changed sharply at the same time as the policy shift.

**What LENS cannot say:** that Lurie caused this. The data is the starting point, not the verdict. An analyst would need to cross-reference encampment clearing dates, officer deployment records, and 311 data to establish the connection.

---

## Other stories in the same data

- **The feedback loop made visible.** SOMA's high raw incident count (Lens 1) has historically been used to justify heavy patrol. Lens 2 shows that much of that "crime" is officer-initiated, not resident-reported — the circular logic that LENS was built to expose.
- **The clearance gap.** If burglary resolution rates in SOMA are lower than citywide even as proactive enforcement surges, that is the enforcement-vs-service tension in one neighborhood — more stops, worse outcomes for victims.
- **Mission disaggregated.** Mission has both a large Latino community and a large homeless population. The +43 spike there is a separate story from SOMA and worth disaggregating by crime type once Lens 3 (Resolution Gap) is built.
- **World Cup 2026.** The same analysis applied to May–Jul 2026 would show whether enforcement shifted toward tourist/stadium areas. This is the natural second chapter — same tool, same method, live policy question.

## Demo script (two-click story)

1. Open LENS → switch to Lens 2 (Police Stops vs. Crime Reports)
2. Click "Before / After" → select preset "Mayor Lurie takes office"
3. Map recolors: SOMA and Mission go deep red, Tenderloin stays moderate
4. Click South of Market → panel shows +79.0 delta
5. Say: *"This is not a crime spike. This is an enforcement spike. The number of burglaries reported by residents barely moved. What moved is how many drug stops and warrant checks officers made — and it moved in exactly the neighborhoods where the mayor ordered encampment clearings."*
6. Switch preset to "World Cup SF (2026)" → *"And here's the same question for a different policy moment — we're watching it in real time."*
